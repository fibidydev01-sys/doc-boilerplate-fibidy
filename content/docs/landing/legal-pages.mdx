The landing module ships **5 legal page templates** at `/legal/*`. Each one describes data flows that genuinely exist in this boilerplate (Supabase, Lemon Squeezy, Resend, AES-256-GCM encrypted credentials, signed webhooks). They're a starting point for your own publication.

> ⚠️ **Templates, not legal advice.** Every jurisdiction has different requirements. Have a qualified lawyer review and adjust before publishing in production. Don't assume "boilerplate language" is safe — wrong terms can be worse than missing terms.

## 5 Pages Shipped

| Slug | Title | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| `privacy-policy` | Privacy Policy | What data is collected, third-party processors, encryption at rest, user rights |
| `terms` | Terms of Service | Account creation, acceptable use, subscriptions, refunds, disclaimers, governing law |
| `license` | End-User License | Access grant, restrictions, ownership of the running service |
| `disclaimer` | Disclaimer | No-warranty, third-party limits, no professional advice |
| `acceptable-use` | Acceptable Use Policy | What's allowed, prohibited activity, security disclosure, abuse reporting |

All five are linked from the footer + the legal sidebar. The sidebar auto-renders from `legalPageOrder` in `content/legal/index.ts`.

## What Each Template Describes

### Privacy Policy

Maps directly to the data flows in the codebase:

- **Account data** — email + chosen display name, plus OAuth profile data when signing in with Google
- **Activity logs** — append-only audit trail (login, logout, signup, profile changes, integration changes); the actual `activity_logs` table that ships with the boilerplate
- **Encrypted credentials** — Lemon Squeezy API keys + webhook secrets encrypted with AES-256-GCM before storage; key kept server-side
- **Cookies** — Supabase auth session cookie (strictly necessary), locale preference cookie. **No advertising cookies, no third-party trackers.**
- **Third-party processors** — Supabase (database/auth/storage), Lemon Squeezy (commerce), Resend (transactional email)
- **User rights** — access, correction, deletion, objection, portability — phrased generically so the same template works for GDPR, CCPA, UU PDP

If your fork adds new data collection (analytics, file uploads, search history, push subscriptions), update this template accordingly.

### Terms of Service

Standard SaaS terms, adapted to what the boilerplate actually does:

- **Account creation** — references email/password, Google OAuth, magic link as actual options
- **Acceptable use** — references the Acceptable Use Policy (separate page) for the full list
- **Subscriptions and billing** — explicitly mentions Lemon Squeezy as the payment processor; clarifies that **Lemon Squeezy is the merchant of record** (LS handles tax compliance, not you)
- **Refunds** — refers to point-of-purchase terms; you fill in your actual refund policy
- **Disclaimers + limitation of liability** — capped at the greater of (a) 12 months of fees paid, or (b) USD 100. Adjust the number for your business model.
- **Governing law** — uses `NEXT_PUBLIC_APP_LEGAL_JURISDICTION` env var. Set it to your real jurisdiction (e.g. `"Delaware, United States"`, `"Singapore"`, `"England and Wales"`, `"Indonesia"`).

### End-User License

The license that governs **end-user access to your deployed product**. This is **NOT** the license under which the underlying boilerplate source code was distributed to you — that's a separate concern between you and whoever sold you the boilerplate.

What it does:

- Grants the user a limited, non-exclusive, non-transferable right to access the service
- Prohibits resale, sublicense, reverse-engineering, scraping for ML training, account sharing
- Defers payment + ownership to the Terms of Service

Important: edit this **before** publishing. The default copy is conservative; loosen restrictions if your product is open-source, freely sharable, or licensed differently.

### Disclaimer

Catches the category of legal protections that don't fit cleanly into ToS:

- No warranty (as-is, as-available)
- Not professional advice (legal, financial, tax, medical)
- Third-party service limits (Supabase / Lemon Squeezy / Resend outages aren't your fault)
- External links (you're not responsible for what other sites do)
- User-generated content disclaimer
- No guarantee of results

Most SaaS apps need this even when the Terms of Service exists separately. Some jurisdictions require an explicit disclaimer.

### Acceptable Use Policy

Concrete list of what users can and can't do:

- **Allowed** — one account per person/org, lawful purposes, connecting integrations, submitting content you have rights to
- **Prohibited (general)** — fraud, malware, spam, harassment, IP infringement, privacy violations
- **Prohibited (technical)** — reverse-engineering, vulnerability probing without permission, circumventing rate limits, **forging or replaying webhook signatures**, scraping, ML training on the service
- **Account integrity** — keep credentials confidential, no sharing across users when the plan doesn't allow
- **Connected payment integrations** — comply with the connected provider's (Lemon Squeezy's) acceptable use too
- **Reporting + security disclosure** — channels for reporting abuse and disclosing vulnerabilities responsibly

The "technical abuse" section is more specific than most templates because the codebase has clear technical surfaces (HMAC-verified webhooks, encrypted credentials, rate-limited endpoints). Tweak based on what your fork actually exposes.

## Architecture

### Content Files

Each legal page is a typed object in `content/legal/<slug>.ts`:

```ts
// content/legal/privacy-policy.ts
export const privacyPolicyContent: LegalPage = {
  slug: "privacy-policy",
  title: "Privacy Policy",
  lastUpdated: new Date().toISOString().slice(0, 10),
  intro: ["Paragraph 1", "Paragraph 2"],
  sections: [
    {
      heading: "What we collect",
      paragraphs: ["..."],
      list: { type: "bulleted", items: ["..."] },
      subsections: [/* nested */],
    },
    // ...
  ],
  contact: {
    name: "{appName} Privacy",
    email: brandingConfig.supportEmail,
  },
};
```

The `LegalPage` type supports paragraphs, bulleted/numbered lists, and recursive subsections — enough to express most legal documents without HTML.

### Renderer

A single component (`<LegalContent>`) renders any `LegalPage` object:

```tsx
// src/app/(marketing)/legal/privacy-policy/page.tsx
import { LegalContent, interpolateBrand, privacyPolicyContent } from "@/modules/landing";
import { brandingConfig } from "@/config";

export default function PrivacyPolicyPage() {
  const page = interpolateBrand(privacyPolicyContent, brandingConfig.name);
  return <LegalContent page={page} />;
}
```

`{appName}` is interpolated. The renderer handles paragraphs, lists, subsections, and the contact footer automatically.

### Sidebar

`<LegalSidebar />` reads from a registry to render nav links:

```ts
// content/legal/index.ts
export const legalPages = {
  "privacy-policy": privacyPolicyContent,
  terms: termsContent,
  license: licenseContent,
  disclaimer: disclaimerContent,
  "acceptable-use": acceptableUseContent,
};

export const legalPageOrder: Array<keyof typeof legalPages> = [
  "privacy-policy",
  "terms",
  "license",
  "disclaimer",
  "acceptable-use",
];
```

Add to `legalPageOrder` to control nav order. Drop a slug from the array to hide a page from the sidebar (the route still resolves if visited directly).

## Last-Updated Date

Each template uses `new Date().toISOString().slice(0, 10)` — the date is set at **build time**, not authoring time. Every production deploy refreshes the "Last updated" date.

This is convenient (no manual date bumping) but slightly inaccurate — the date moves even if no legal text changed. Two ways to make it precise:

1. **Hardcode the date** in each content file. Bump manually when you actually edit:
   ```ts
   lastUpdated: "2026-04-25",
   ```

2. **Track per-section history** in a separate audit log if your jurisdiction requires it (rare for general SaaS).

For most products, the build-time approach is fine.

## Adding a New Legal Page

Three steps:

### 1. Create the content file

```ts
// content/legal/cookie-policy.ts
import { brandingConfig } from "@/config";
import type { LegalPage } from "../../types";

export const cookiePolicyContent: LegalPage = {
  slug: "cookie-policy",
  title: "Cookie Policy",
  lastUpdated: new Date().toISOString().slice(0, 10),
  intro: ["..."],
  sections: [
    {
      heading: "What cookies we use",
      paragraphs: ["..."],
    },
    // ...
  ],
  contact: {
    name: "{appName} Privacy",
    email: brandingConfig.supportEmail,
  },
};
```

### 2. Register in the index

```ts
// content/legal/index.ts
import { cookiePolicyContent } from "./cookie-policy";

export { cookiePolicyContent };

export const legalPages = {
  "privacy-policy": privacyPolicyContent,
  terms: termsContent,
  license: licenseContent,
  disclaimer: disclaimerContent,
  "acceptable-use": acceptableUseContent,
  "cookie-policy": cookiePolicyContent,                    // ← add
};

export const legalPageOrder = [
  "privacy-policy",
  "cookie-policy",                                         // ← add in desired position
  "terms",
  "license",
  "disclaimer",
  "acceptable-use",
] as const;
```

### 3. Create the route file

```tsx
// src/app/(marketing)/legal/cookie-policy/page.tsx
import type { Metadata } from "next";
import { brandingConfig } from "@/config";
import {
  LegalContent,
  cookiePolicyContent,
  interpolateBrand,
} from "@/modules/landing";

export const metadata: Metadata = {
  title: cookiePolicyContent.title,
};

export default function CookiePolicyPage() {
  const page = interpolateBrand(cookiePolicyContent, brandingConfig.name);
  return <LegalContent page={page} />;
}
```

The sidebar picks up the new page automatically. Update the footer columns in `content/layout.ts` if you want it in the footer.

## Removing a Legal Page

If you don't need one of the defaults (e.g. your product doesn't have an Acceptable Use policy because it's a single-tenant tool):

1. Remove from `legalPageOrder` and the `legalPages` map in `content/legal/index.ts`
2. Remove the export in the same file
3. Delete the route file `src/app/(marketing)/legal/<slug>/page.tsx`
4. Optionally delete the content file `content/legal/<slug>.ts`
5. Remove the link from `content/layout.ts` footer columns

The route returns 404 if you skip step 3 but keep step 1.

## Pre-Publish Checklist

Before going live with these pages:

- [ ] **Lawyer review** — at minimum the Privacy Policy and Terms (the other three are usually less risky but still worth reviewing)
- [ ] `NEXT_PUBLIC_APP_LEGAL_JURISDICTION` set to your real jurisdiction (used in Terms governing law clause)
- [ ] `NEXT_PUBLIC_APP_SUPPORT_EMAIL` is monitored — these pages publish your contact address
- [ ] If your product collects data not described in the default Privacy Policy (analytics, push subscriptions, file uploads, location), update the template
- [ ] If your product has compliance requirements (HIPAA, PCI-DSS, GDPR Article 30 records), the templates are NOT sufficient — work with a specialist
- [ ] If you're shipping a different license (open-source, freemium with paid tier), rewrite the End-User License from scratch
- [ ] Refund policy in Terms matches what you actually do (the default refers to "point-of-purchase terms" without specifics)
